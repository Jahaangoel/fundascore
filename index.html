How the score is built

FundaScore isn't a black box. Every step of the calculation is transparent and designed to be explainable.

// Step 1 — Normalize each metric to 0–100
S_ROE = clamp( (ROE + 10) / 35 × 100, 0, 100 )
S_EPS = clamp( (EPS_growth + 20) / 50 × 100, 0, 100 )
S_RG = clamp( (Rev_growth + 20) / 50 × 100, 0, 100 )
S_PE = clamp( (80 − PE) / 72 × 100, 0, 100 ) // inverted
S_PH = clamp( (Promoter − 10) / 65 × 100, 0, 100 )

// Step 2 — Weighted composite score
FundaScore = 0.30×S_ROE + 0.25×S_EPS + 0.20×S_RG + 0.15×S_PE + 0.10×S_PH
Step 1
Normalization
Each metric lives on a different scale — ROE is a percentage, P/E is a ratio, revenue growth can be negative. Normalization converts all five to a 0–100 score using defined benchmarks so they can be combined fairly.
Step 2
Inversion for P/E
For most metrics, higher is better. P/E is the exception — a lower P/E generally means a more attractively valued stock. The formula inverts P/E so a P/E of 8 scores 100 and a P/E of 80 scores 0.
Step 3
Clamping
If a value falls outside the benchmark range (e.g. ROE of 40%), it gets clamped to 100. This prevents extreme outliers from distorting the score while still rewarding strong performance.
Step 4
Weighted sum
Each normalized score is multiplied by its weight — ROE carries the most (30%) because it directly measures capital efficiency. The five weighted scores are summed to produce the final FundaScore out of 100.
Metrics guide

What each metric tells you

Each of the five metrics measures a different dimension of a company's financial health. Here's what to look for.

Return on equity (ROE)
30%

Measures how much profit the company generates for every rupee of shareholder equity. A consistently high ROE signals a business that compounds value efficiently. Warren Buffett's favourite metric for a reason.

−10% (worst)25%+ (best)
EPS growth (YoY)
25%

Year-over-year growth in earnings per share. This reflects whether the company is actually becoming more profitable over time. Consistent EPS growth is harder to fake than a one-time earnings spike.

−20% (worst)30%+ (best)
Revenue growth (YoY)
20%

Growth in top-line revenue year over year. Revenue is harder to manipulate than earnings, which makes it a reliable health indicator. A company with no revenue growth has a ceiling on long-term EPS growth too.

−20% (worst)30%+ (best)
P/E ratio
15%

Price-to-earnings ratio — how much the market pays per rupee of earnings. Lower is generally more attractive from a valuation standpoint. Note: P/E is context-dependent — a high P/E in a growth sector may be justified.

8 (best)80+ (worst)
Promoter holding
10%

The percentage of shares held by the founders or promoters of the company. High promoter holding signals that those who built the business still have skin in the game — a confidence indicator, not a performance metric.

10% (worst)75%+ (best)
